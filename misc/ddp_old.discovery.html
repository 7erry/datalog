Why this matters

Non-competitive paths—staying on ad hoc tools, fragmented stacks, or “we’ll fix it later” designs—compound as rework, incidents, and stalled decisions. The sections below name how status-quo choices fail and what delay actually costs.

Discovery Operating Framework

Before the call

Pre-Meeting Planner + TAPO: Define 1-3 SMART objectives, your hypothesis about the customer's current before-state, and open with TAPO (Time, Attendees, Plan, Outcome) so success criteria are explicit from minute one.

Anchor scope: Confirm workload, environment stage (dev or prod), whether MongoDB is already selected, and your provisional Tiger Route (Classic, Sprint, or Fast) to align depth and expected next step.

During the call

Cover the 5Ps deliberately: People (org map and signer), Project (workload and business context), Pain/Priority (including why now vs later), and Process (evaluation steps, stakeholders, alternatives).

Make the business case explicit: Tie value drivers to measurable Positive Business Outcomes (compete/make money, save money, reduce risk, accelerate time-to-value) with owners and a real compelling date (no date, no deal).

End of call

Close on NBM + MAP: Co-create the Next Business Meeting and a Mutual Action Plan with agenda, owners, dates, and decision criteria. Avoid a vague we will follow up close.

After the call

Discovery Capture Sheet: Update account, workload, motion, value drivers, timeline, evaluation process, Tiger Route, and open gaps. Consolidate notes into this as the single source of truth before follow-on discovery.

Discovery: MongoDB Atlas — Developer Data Platform (earlier narrative)

Companion: ddp_old.html

Opening minute (discovery call)

Thanks—this is an earlier take on the Developer Data Platform story; it’s useful if you want to see how the narrative evolved or compare messaging. For discovery, I care more about your current constraints: team skills, existing data stores, and compliance boundaries. Are you comparing Atlas to a best-of-breed stack, or trying to consolidate?

Why current approaches fall short (without a competitive move)

Older narratives still shape decisions: teams standardize on stories instead of current capabilities, or dismiss consolidation as marketing. Non-competitive is static mental models—architecture reviews cite slides from two years ago.

Cost of inaction

You mis-scope pilots (missing features you didn’t know existed) or over-build (replicating platform value in-house). Strategy meetings debate ghosts while renewal clocks tick. Education debt turns into wrong RFPs and delayed migrations.

Ten open-ended questions (pain points & buying process)

  1. What has changed in your data strategy since you last looked at MongoDB’s platform story?
  2. Which parts of the stack are you actively trying to consolidate, and which are intentionally separate?
  3. What messaging resonates with your executives vs your engineers—and where do they disagree?
  4. How do you align roadmap bets with vendor roadmaps when priorities shift?
  5. What proof do internal stakeholders need before expanding footprint—cost model, risk assessment, reference architectures?
  6. Who owns vendor relationships—central cloud team, data platform, or line-of-business IT?
  7. What procurement vehicles do you prefer—direct, reseller, marketplace—and why?
  8. What would trigger a fresh bake-off or architecture review this year?
  9. How do you measure whether a platform bet is paying off—velocity, incidents, TCO?
  10. What concerns do people raise quietly in hallway conversations that rarely make the RFP?
