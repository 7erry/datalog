# Discovery: Atlas Search vs specialty engines

**Companion:** `speciality.html`

## Opening minute (discovery call)

Appreciate you joining. This is the broader **specialty search and vector** landscape—Elasticsearch, Pinecone, Qdrant—against a unified Atlas approach. I’ll keep it grounded in **workflow**: how many systems you sync, who operates them, and what breaks at scale. Are you optimizing for **best-of-breed**, or **platform consolidation**?

## Why current approaches fall short (without a competitive move)

Best-of-breed search + vector stacks often mean **five vendors and four sync paths**—each with its own failure domain. Non-competitive is optimizing components while **system reliability degrades**.

## Cost of inaction

You hemorrhage **engineering time** on glue, spend multiples on **egress and ops**, and struggle to enforce **consistent governance**. Strategic agility drops because every feature crosses **too many boundaries**.

## Ten open-ended questions (pain points & buying process)

1. Which specialty tools are in production today, and what job is each doing?
2. Where does the multi-tool stack hurt most—cost, reliability, data drift, hiring?
3. Who funds search and AI infrastructure—central platform, ML org, product P&L?
4. What SLAs differ between “nice to have” search and business-critical retrieval?
5. How do you evaluate consolidation risk—feature gaps, migration effort, organizational politics?
6. What security and data residency rules constrain where indexes and vectors may live?
7. Who would champion staying specialized vs simplifying, and why?
8. What procurement vehicles apply—direct vendor, cloud marketplace, enterprise agreement bundles?
9. What decision deadline is real—renewal, product launch, cost takeout target?
10. What would you need to see in a POC to believe one platform can carry the load?
