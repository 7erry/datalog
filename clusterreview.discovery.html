Why this matters

Non-competitive paths—staying on ad hoc tools, fragmented stacks, or “we’ll fix it later” designs—compound as rework, incidents, and stalled decisions. The sections below name how status-quo choices fail and what delay actually costs.

Discovery Operating Framework

Before the call

Pre-Meeting Planner + TAPO: Define 1-3 SMART objectives, your hypothesis about the customer's current before-state, and open with TAPO (Time, Attendees, Plan, Outcome) so success criteria are explicit from minute one.

Anchor scope: Confirm workload, environment stage (dev or prod), whether MongoDB is already selected, and your provisional Tiger Route (Classic, Sprint, or Fast) to align depth and expected next step.

During the call

Cover the 5Ps deliberately: People (org map and signer), Project (workload and business context), Pain/Priority (including why now vs later), and Process (evaluation steps, stakeholders, alternatives).

Make the business case explicit: Tie value drivers to measurable Positive Business Outcomes (compete/make money, save money, reduce risk, accelerate time-to-value) with owners and a real compelling date (no date, no deal).

End of call

Close on NBM + MAP: Co-create the Next Business Meeting and a Mutual Action Plan with agenda, owners, dates, and decision criteria. Avoid a vague we will follow up close.

After the call

Discovery Capture Sheet: Update account, workload, motion, value drivers, timeline, evaluation process, Tiger Route, and open gaps. Consolidate notes into this as the single source of truth before follow-on discovery.

Discovery: MongoDB Atlas health check

Companion: clusterreview.html

Opening minute (discovery call)

Appreciate you joining. Think of this as a structured health pass on Atlas—not a generic “best practices” PDF. We’ll look at signals that predict incidents, surprise bills, and slow queries before they become fires. I want to leave you with a short list of prioritized fixes, not a hundred tickets. Is this cluster mission-critical production, or are we sizing and hardening ahead of a migration?

Why current approaches fall short (without a competitive move)

Without periodic health discipline, clusters drift: indexes nobody dares drop, zombie connections, backup policies never restored, and metrics nobody ties to customer pain. Non-competitive operations mean tribal knowledge and heroics—not a prioritized risk backlog.

Cost of inaction

Inaction converts to preventable incidents, overtime, and renewals negotiated from weakness because reliability stories are thin. Performance regressions become “MongoDB is slow” narratives instead of fixable design issues. You pay for fire drills that a structured review would have flagged as yellow weeks earlier.

Ten open-ended questions (pain points & buying process)

  1. What symptoms are you seeing—latency, errors, cost spikes, failed deploys—and how do users experience them?
  2. How do you monitor MongoDB today—Atlas UI, APM, logs, custom metrics—and where are the blind spots?
  3. When did you last run a restore or failover drill, and what did you learn?
  4. Who owns remediation—DBA, platform, app teams—and how do escalations work?
  5. What change-management rules slow fixes down, and which ones you consider non-negotiable?
  6. Is there budget or appetite for reserved tiers, consulting, or training tied to this review?
  7. What would define success from this engagement—a report, fixed issues, or a roadmap?
  8. Are there upcoming events—launches, holidays, audits—that raise the stakes?
  9. How do you prioritize technical debt vs feature work when both compete for the same team?
  10. What would make your leadership confident MongoDB is “under control” for the next year?
