Why this matters

Non-competitive paths—staying on ad hoc tools, fragmented stacks, or “we’ll fix it later” designs—compound as rework, incidents, and stalled decisions. The sections below name how status-quo choices fail and what delay actually costs.

Discovery Operating Framework

Before the call

Pre-Meeting Planner + TAPO: Define 1-3 SMART objectives, your hypothesis about the customer's current before-state, and open with TAPO (Time, Attendees, Plan, Outcome) so success criteria are explicit from minute one.

Anchor scope: Confirm workload, environment stage (dev or prod), whether MongoDB is already selected, and your provisional Tiger Route (Classic, Sprint, or Fast) to align depth and expected next step.

During the call

Cover the 5Ps deliberately: People (org map and signer), Project (workload and business context), Pain/Priority (including why now vs later), and Process (evaluation steps, stakeholders, alternatives).

Make the business case explicit: Tie value drivers to measurable Positive Business Outcomes (compete/make money, save money, reduce risk, accelerate time-to-value) with owners and a real compelling date (no date, no deal).

End of call

Close on NBM + MAP: Co-create the Next Business Meeting and a Mutual Action Plan with agenda, owners, dates, and decision criteria. Avoid a vague we will follow up close.

After the call

Discovery Capture Sheet: Update account, workload, motion, value drivers, timeline, evaluation process, Tiger Route, and open gaps. Consolidate notes into this as the single source of truth before follow-on discovery.

Discovery: AI search platform comparison

Companion: aisearch.html

Opening minute (discovery call)

Thanks—this is a vendor and architecture conversation as much as a feature one. Teams evaluating managed AI search usually care about relevance, connectors, security boundaries, and what it costs when usage spikes. I’ll frame this as an honest comparison: where each option wins, where it gets expensive, and what you’d still own operationally. To set the table: are you replacing something already in production, or greenfield for a new product?

Why current approaches fall short (without a competitive move)

Status quo is often three products pretending to be one: search, LLM gateway, and content connectors stitched with scripts. Procurement buys “AI search” but operations inherit opaque relevance, weak governance, and unpredictable metering. Non-competitive evaluations compare slide decks instead of your corpus, your compliance, your latency SLOs.

Cost of inaction

Inaction shows up as pilot purgatory: promising demos that can’t pass enterprise gates or survive cost at scale. Support and internal users churn when answers are confident but wrong. You burn quarters reconciling logging, access control, and data residency across vendors that weren’t chosen as a system.

Ten open-ended questions (pain points & buying process)

  1. What problem are you solving with AI search that your current search or support stack isn’t solving?
  2. Describe the content corpus—size, freshness, sensitivity, and who ingests it today.
  3. What broke or worried you in past search or ML projects—cost, relevance, governance, or delivery time?
  4. Who uses the outcome—customers, employees, agents—and what does success look like in their words?
  5. How do you compare vendors today—POCs, bake-offs, security questionnaires, or reference calls?
  6. What non-negotiables came out of InfoSec or legal for indexing and query logging?
  7. Who controls budget for search infrastructure, and is it OpEx, project-based, or tied to a cloud commit?
  8. What’s your timeline, and what business event is driving the date?
  9. Which stakeholders could stall a decision even if engineering loves the tech?
  10. After you choose a platform, who operates it day to day and what skills gaps worry you?
