**Companion:** `cts.html`

## Opening minute (discovery call)

Appreciate the conversation. This is framed around **AI-powered operations**—the kind of program where observability, automation, and governance have to meet enterprise reality. I’ll connect the narrative to **outcomes**: fewer Sev-1s, faster mean-time-to-understand, and safer change. Who’s the primary buyer here—**platform engineering**, **SRE**, or **a business unit** sponsoring the initiative?

## Why current approaches fall short (without a competitive move)

AI ops initiatives fail when they’re **dashboards without workflow**: alerts still route to humans with no context, and automation lacks **guardrails**. Non-competitive is buying “AIOps” as branding while runbooks stay static and data stays siloed.

## Cost of inaction

Inaction preserves **toil and MTTR** that executives thought they funded away. Skepticism grows after a flashy POC can’t pass **change control**. You waste budget on tools that don’t connect to **tickets, CMDB, and actionable remediation**—so value never reaches the incident bridge.

## Ten open-ended questions (pain points & buying process)

1. What operational pain is most visible to leadership—outages, slow releases, audit findings, or cost?
2. How do incidents get diagnosed today, and where do teams lose time in the first thirty minutes?
3. What data sources must an AI ops solution respect—logs, metrics, traces, CMDB, tickets?
4. Who owns the roadmap for automation vs human-in-the-loop workflows?
5. What guardrails does security require before AI can suggest or execute changes?
6. How do you measure ROI for ops tooling—MTTR, toil reduction, customer impact?
7. What procurement path does this take—SI engagement, software license, cloud marketplace?
8. Who are the executive sponsors, and what outcome do they need this quarter vs next year?
9. What failed or stalled “AIOps” efforts are people still skeptical about?
10. What would a phased rollout look like that builds trust instead of resistance?
